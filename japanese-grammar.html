How to read this

A reference, not a course

Skim the section labels in the top nav; the rules are dense but the examples are short. Romaji is included for the kana sections only — past that it gets in the way.

Japanese grammar is built on a few small primitives — three verb groups, two adjective types, a small set of stems — combined by attaching endings to those stems. Once you can identify which group a verb is in and find its stems, the rest of the system unfolds mechanically.

This reference is organized around that idea. §1 Writing covers the scripts. §2 Verbs is the structural core — verb groups, the stems, and every form derived from them. §3 Particles covers the eleven that account for almost all sentence-marking work. §4 Adjectives and §5 Patterns fill in modifiers and sentence construction. §6 Keigo sketches the three politeness registers, and §7 Reference is the conjugation matrix and decision trees.

★ Stems
Find the right one
Most conjugation is "attach X to stem Y." Know your -i, -a, -e, -o, -te stems and 80% of grammar follows.
★ Te-form
The structural verb
The te-form connects, lists, requests, permits, prohibits, and forms the progressive. Memorize the rule that derives it from the dictionary form.
★ Particles
Roles, not prepositions
Particles mark a noun's role in the sentence (topic, subject, object, etc.) rather than translating English prepositions one-to-one.
書
§ 一 · Writing

書Writing & Pronunciation

Japanese is written in three scripts used together. Hiragana writes native words and grammar; katakana writes foreign borrowings and emphasis; kanji writes content roots. Pronunciation is strictly mora-based — each kana is one beat.

1.1

音Sounds & mora

Japanese has 5 vowels — a i u e o — pronounced as in Spanish or Italian. Consonants (except n) always pair with a vowel into a single mora. The double-consonant marker っ and the long-vowel marker (ー or doubled vowel) each count as one mora. Pitch accent distinguishes some words (橋 hashi↓ "bridge" vs. 箸 ↑hashi "chopsticks") but is rarely written.

1.2

仮名Hiragana — ひらがな

The base 46 syllables. Add ゛ (dakuten) for voiced consonants — か→が, た→だ, は→ば — or ゜ (handakuten) for the p-row: は→ぱ. Combinations like きゃ・きゅ・きょ form one mora.

Particle spellings

Three particles are spelled with kana that don't match their pronunciation: topic は is read wa, direction へ is read e, and object を is read o. The pronunciation is purely positional — when these kana appear as particles, they shift; everywhere else は is ha and へ is he.

1.3

片仮名Katakana — カタカナ

Same syllable inventory, used for loanwords, foreign names, onomatopoeia, and emphasis (a bit like italics). The long-vowel marker ー appears almost exclusively in katakana.

コーヒー · アメリカ · ヴィクトル
kōhī (coffee) · amerika (America) · Vikutoru (Victor)

Extended katakana handles foreign sounds that hiragana can't: ファ・フィ・フェ・フォ for f-, ティ・ディ for ti / di, ヴァ・ヴィ・ヴ・ヴェ・ヴォ for v-.

1.4

漢字Kanji

Chinese-origin logographs. Most kanji carry two reading types: 音読み (on-yomi), the Chinese-derived reading used mostly in compounds; and 訓読み (kun-yomi), the native Japanese reading used for standalone words.

KanjiOn-yomiKun-yomiStandaloneIn compound
人ジン · ニンひと人 hito — person日本人 nihon-jin — Japanese person
山サンやま山 yama — mountain富士山 fuji-san — Mt. Fuji
食ショクた(べる)食べる taberu — to eat食事 shoku-ji — meal

Okurigana are the trailing hiragana that show inflection: 食べる writes the root 食 in kanji and the verb ending べる in hiragana. The okurigana changes as the verb conjugates (食べた · 食べて · 食べない); the kanji doesn't.

§ 二 · Verbs

動Verbs

All verbs end in a u-row syllable in the dictionary form. They split into three groups; the group determines how every other form is derived. The two irregular verbs する and 来る appear everywhere.

2.1

分類The three groups

五段Group 1 — godan / u-verbs

Ends in any u-row kana except る, OR ends in る preceded by an a/u/o-row vowel. Examples: 書く kaku, 話す hanasu, 飲む nomu, 取る toru.

一段Group 2 — ichidan / ru-verbs

Ends in る preceded by an i- or e-row vowel. Examples: 食べる taberu, 見る miru, 寝る neru.

不規則Group 3 — irregular

Only two members in everyday use: する (to do — appears in hundreds of compound verbs like 勉強する) and 来る kuru (to come). A few semi-irregulars exist, notably 行く iku (treated as godan except in te/ta where it becomes 行って・行った) and ある (godan, but its negative is the irregular ない).

The disguise problem

A verb ending in る with a/u/o before it is always Group 1. But verbs ending in る with i/e before it can be either group — most are Group 2, but a handful are Group 1 in disguise: 帰る kaeru (return), 切る kiru (cut), 走る hashiru (run), 入る hairu (enter), 知る shiru (know), 要る iru (need). Memorize these as Group 1 exceptions.

2.2

辞書形Dictionary form & the five stems

The dictionary form is plain non-past — used for the present tense, future, and as the citation form. Every other form is built by replacing the final syllable with a different vowel-row syllable. These are the stems.

-i (連用)書き (kak-i)食べpolite (-ます), noun form, conjunction
-u (終止)書く (kak-u)食べるdictionary form / plain non-past
-e (仮定)書け (kak-e)食べれconditional (-ば), potential (G1)

For Group 2, the stem is just the verb minus る, used for everything. Group 1's "stem" depends on the form — each ending picks its own vowel row.

2.3

2.4

普通形Plain (dictionary) form & plain past

The plain form is used in casual speech, with close friends and family, and (importantly) inside subordinate clauses regardless of overall politeness. Plain past adds -た via the same rule as the te-form.

AffirmativeNegative
Non-past食べる · 書く食べない · 書かない
Past食べた · 書いた食べなかった · 書かなかった

Negative formation: Group 1 takes -a stem + ない (書く → 書かない); Group 2 drops る and adds ない (食べる → 食べない); irregular する → しない, 来る → こない. Exception: ある (to exist) is negative ない, not *あらない.

2.5

テ形The te-form — the most important form

The te-form derives from the dictionary form via a simple rule per ending. Once you have it, replacing the final て with た gives the plain past. Memorizing the te-form rule pays for itself a hundred times over.

〜ている vs. 〜てある

〜ている = ongoing action or resulting state. 〜てある = the result of someone's deliberate action remaining visible. Compare 窓が開いている (the window is open — just describing the state) with 窓が開けてある (the window has been opened — implying someone did it for a reason).

2.6

時制・相Tense & aspect

Japanese has two tenses (past, non-past) and a small aspect system layered on the te-form. The non-past covers both present habitual and future.

FormMeaningExample
非過去 plain non-pasthabitual / future毎日コーヒーを飲む。
過去 plain pastcompleted action昨日映画を見た。
〜ているprogressive (active verb)今食べている。
〜ているresulting state (change-of-state verb)結婚している · 知っている · 死んでいる
〜ていたpast progressive電話していた。
〜たことがあるhave experienced日本に行ったことがあります。

The 知っている trap

Some verbs describe a momentary change-of-state, and 〜ている with them means "the state resulting from that change." 知る "come to know" → 知っている "know (currently)." Negative: 知りません "I don't know," not *知っていません. Other examples: 結婚する → 結婚している (be married), 死ぬ → 死んでいる (be dead).

2.7

受身・使役Passive, causative, potential

All three are built on the -a stem for Group 1, on the bare root for Group 2.

VoiceGroup 1 endingGroup 2 endingExample
Passive 〜される/られる-a + れる+ られる書かれる · 食べられる · される · 来られる
Causative 〜させる-a + せる+ させる書かせる · 食べさせる · させる · 来させる
Causative-passive 〜させられる-a + せられる / -a + される+ させられる書かせられる / 書かされる · 食べさせられる
Potential 〜られる / 〜える-e + る+ られる書ける · 食べられる · できる · 来られる

Passive — affected by an action. The performer takes に:

弟にケーキを食べられた。
My (little) brother ate my cake (on me).

Causative — make/let someone do. The do-er takes に (let) or を (make):

子供に野菜を食べさせた。
I made/let the child eat vegetables.

Causative-passive — being made to do, often unwillingly.

妹に部屋を掃除させられた。
I was made (by my sister) to clean the room.

Potential — be able to do. Object often takes が:

日本語が話せる。
I can speak Japanese.
"ら-nuki" speech

In casual modern speech, Group 2 potentials often drop the ら: 食べられる → 食べれる, 見られる → 見れる. Widely used but still considered nonstandard in formal writing.

2.8

条件Conditionals — the four forms

Japanese has four distinct conditionals, each with its own nuance. Choosing the right one matters more than you'd expect.

FormFormationUseExample
〜とplain non-past + とnatural / inevitable result; habitual春になると、桜が咲く。
When spring comes, the cherry blossoms bloom.
〜ばverb -e stem + ば; い-adj. drop い + ければgeneral / hypothetical "if"時間があれば、行きます。
If I have time, I'll go.
〜たらpast form + らspecific event / "when/after"; most flexible家に帰ったら、電話します。
When I get home, I'll call.
〜ならplain form + なら"if it's the case that"; topic-conditional日本に行くなら、京都がいい。
If you're going to Japan, Kyoto is good.
Choosing the right conditional

If the result is automatic or law-like, use と. If it's hypothetical/general, use ば. If it's "after this specific thing happens," use たら. If you're responding to information someone gave you ("if that's the case…"), use なら. たら is the most flexible and the safe default in casual speech.

2.9

意志Volitional, imperative, prohibitive

FormPolitePlainMeaning
Volitional食べましょう食べよう · 書こうlet's / I shall
Imperative食べてください食べろ · 書けdo! (plain is forceful)
Prohibitive食べないでください食べるな · 書くなdon't!
Soft suggestion食べたらどう?食べたら?why don't you eat?

Plain imperatives (食べろ・書け) are blunt — used in shouted commands, sports, anger, or close-male speech. The standard polite request is 〜てください; the more polite is 〜ていただけませんか.

2.10

授受Giving & receiving — あげる, くれる, もらう

Three verbs encode the direction of giving in Japanese. The choice depends on who is the in-group relative to the speaker.

VerbDirectionPatternExample
あげるI/in-group → others (outward)A は B に X を あげる私は友達に本をあげた。
くれるothers → me/in-group (inward)A は B(=me) に X を くれる友達が私に本をくれた。
もらうI/in-group ← others (receiving)A は B から/に X を もらう私は友達に本をもらった。

The same three verbs attach to the te-form to express favor-doing: 〜てあげる "do for someone (outward)," 〜てくれる "do for me (inward)," 〜てもらう "have/get someone to do for me."

道を教えてくれてありがとう。
Thanks for showing me the way (lit. for showing-me-as-favor).

Polite forms: 差し上げる (humble give to a superior), くださる (honorific give to me from a superior), いただく (humble receive from a superior).

§ 三 · Particles

助Particles — 助詞

Particles attach after a noun (or noun phrase) to mark its grammatical role. They're not prepositions — they describe what the noun does in the sentence, not where things are relative to each other. The eleven listed here account for the vast majority of usage.

3.1

3.2

は対がThe は vs. が problem

The most-asked question in Japanese grammar. A starting heuristic, not an absolute rule:

Use は when…

· The noun is the topic (already known or introduced).
· You're contrasting two things.
· The sentence makes a general statement.
· In negative sentences, the predicate's focus often takes は.

Use が when…

· The noun is new information.
· You're identifying (answering "who/what?").
· Inside a subordinate clause (relative clauses, は often turns into が).
· With questions about subject (誰が…?).
· Describing states/feelings (好き, 上手, 分かる, ある).

Test it

"There is a cat in the garden" (introducing) → 庭に猫がいます。 "The cat is in the garden" (about the cat) → 猫は庭にいます。 Same scene, different starting point.

3.3

に対でに vs. で — location split

Both mark location, but for different verbs:

  • に — location of existence or destination. Used with stative verbs (いる・ある・住む・座る) and verbs of arrival (行く・来る・着く・入る). 部屋にいる · 東京に住む。
  • で — location where an action happens. Used with action verbs (食べる・勉強する・働く). 図書館で勉強する。

§ 四 · Adjectives

形Adjectives — 形容詞

Two types: i-adjectives (which conjugate like a kind of verb) and na-adjectives (which behave like nouns and take な to modify another noun). Knowing the type tells you how to make every form.

4.1

い形容詞i-adjectives

Always end in い in the dictionary form (大きい, おいしい, 高い). They conjugate by replacing or appending to that final い — the copula です is just added on top for politeness, never inflects with the adjective itself.

FormPatternExample (大きい)
Non-past affirmative—大きい(です)
Non-past negativedrop い + くない大きくない(です)
Past affirmativedrop い + かった大きかった(です)
Past negativedrop い + くなかった大きくなかった(です)
Adverbialdrop い + く大きく書く (write big-ly)
Te-form (connector)drop い + くて大きくて、新しい
Modifying a nounjust attach大きい本
いい is irregular

The adjective いい (good) conjugates from its older form よい. So: よくない (not good), よかった (was good), よくなかった (was not good). Never *いくない or *いかった.

4.2

な形容詞na-adjectives

End in any sound, but require な when directly modifying a noun: きれいな花. They inflect via the copula (だ・です・じゃない・だった) just like nouns.

FormPlainPoliteExample (静か)
Non-past affirmative〜だ〜です静かだ · 静かです
Non-past negative〜じゃない〜じゃないです / 〜ではありません静かじゃない

Past negative〜じゃなかった〜じゃなかったです静かじゃなかった
Adverbial〜に—静かに歩く
Te-form (connector)〜で—静かで、きれいだ
Modifying a noun〜な—静かな部屋
きれい trap

A few na-adjectives look like i-adjectives because they end in い: きれい (pretty), きらい (disliked), 有名 (famous, when written 有名い…it never is — it's just 有名). When in doubt, check: does it take な before a noun? Then it's na.

§ 五 · Sentence Patterns

文Sentence Patterns

The default Japanese word order is Subject-Object-Verb, but really it's topic-comment: the topic comes first (marked with は), then everything else, with the verb almost always at the end. Modifiers always precede what they modify.

5.1

語順Word order & the copula

The basic skeletons:

PatternMeaningExample
N1 は N2 ですN1 is N2私は学生です。
N1 は N2 じゃない / ではないN1 isn't N2私は学生じゃない。
N1 は N2 でしたN1 was N2彼は先生でした。
N1 は Adj-i(no copula needed for plain)この本は高い。
N1 が Adj-na—日本語が上手です。
(N1 は) Vsubject often dropped if clear食べました。

The copula has multiple registers: だ (plain), です (polite), である (literary/declarative), でございます (very polite). The negative is じゃない / ではない (plain) or じゃありません / ではありません (polite).

5.2

存在Existence — ある & いる

"X exists" or "there is X" uses two different verbs depending on what X is:

VerbForExample
いるanimate (people, animals, sometimes spirits)猫がいる。 · 友達がいる。
あるinanimate (objects, plants, abstract things)本がある。 · 時間がある。

Possession uses the same verbs: 車がある "I have a car (it exists for me)," 子供がいる "I have a child." The location particle is に: 部屋に猫がいる.

5.3

修飾Relative clauses

Japanese builds relative clauses by simply putting a verb or adjective phrase directly before the noun it modifies — no equivalent of "who/which/that." The internal subject takes が rather than は.

昨日 買った 本
kinō katta hon
the book (that I) bought yesterday
日本語が上手な人
a person who is good at Japanese
兄が作ったケーキ
the cake (that) my brother made

Relative clauses chain naturally: 私が昨日駅で会った人 "the person I met at the station yesterday."

5.4

名詞化Nominalization — turning verbs into nouns

Two ways to make a verb act as a noun phrase: の and こと. They're often interchangeable, but each has its preferred contexts.

NominalizerWhenExample
〜のconcrete, perceived events; with verbs of perception (見る, 聞く)彼が来るのを見た。
I saw him come.
〜ことabstract concepts, propositions, decisions日本語を勉強することは大切です。

5.6

推量・様態Conjecture & appearance

Japanese has a rich system for hedging certainty. The form you use signals how confident you are and what evidence you have.

FormMeaning & evidenceExample
〜だろう / でしょうprobably (no evidence needed) — speaker's general guess雨が降るでしょう。
〜かもしれないmight / maybe (lower probability)雨が降るかもしれない。
〜はずだshould be / is supposed to be (based on reasoning/expectation)もう着いたはずです。
〜そうだ (-i stem / Adj-i drop い)looks like / about to (visual)雨が降りそうだ。 · おいしそう。
〜ようだ / みたいだseems like (broader evidence; みたいだ is casual)病気のようです。
〜らしいseems / I heard (secondhand or typical)先生は休みらしい。
そう vs. よう vs. らしい

そう is what you see right now ("looks about to rain"). よう is broader inference from circumstances ("she seems sick"). らしい is hearsay or what's typical ("the teacher's apparently off"). With i-adjectives, そう drops the final い (おいしそう), but いい is irregular: よさそう.

§ 六 · Keigo

敬Keigo & Daily Use

Japanese has three formal registers above plain speech, plus a handful of stable expressions you'll hear daily. Mastery is a long road — but recognizing the registers is essential for understanding service workers, formal meetings, and most workplace email.

6.1

敬語The three registers

丁寧語Teineigo — polite

The です・ます register. The default for almost any unfamiliar situation. You should be fluent here before worrying about the other two.

尊敬語Sonkeigo — respectful

Used to elevate the listener or person being talked about (customer, superior, elder). Patterns: お + verb -i stem + になる, special verbs (下記), or passive form (見られる "saw" politely).

謙譲語Kenjōgo — humble

Used to lower yourself or your in-group when speaking with/about an outsider or superior. Patterns: お + verb -i stem + する, or special verbs (下記).

7.2

テ形決定Te-form decision tree

The single most useful rule in Japanese verb morphology. Given any verb in dictionary form, you can derive both te-form (〜て) and plain past (〜た) by following this tree. Past is identical except final て→た, で→だ.

What group is the verb? ├─ Group 2 (ru-verb): drop る, add て │ 食べる → 食べて · 見る → 見て │ ├─ Irregular: │ する → して │ 来る → 来て (kite) │ 行く (godan exception) → 行って ← not 行いて │ └─ Group 1 (godan): look at the LAST KANA of the dictionary form ├─ う · つ · る → 〜って │ 買う → 買って · 待つ → 待って · 取る → 取って │ ├─ む · ぶ · ぬ → 〜んで (note: で, not て) │ 飲む → 飲んで · 遊ぶ → 遊んで · 死ぬ → 死んで │ ├─ く → 〜いて │ 書く → 書いて · 聞く → 聞いて │ ├─ ぐ → 〜いで (note: で, not て) │ 泳ぐ → 泳いで · 急ぐ → 急いで │ └─ す → 〜して 話す → 話して · 貸す → 貸して
For plain past (〜た), use the same tree

Just swap the final て → た, で → だ. So 書いて → 書いた, 飲んで → 飲んだ, 食べて → 食べた, 行って → 行った.

7.6

勉強A study suggestion

If you're using this as a reference during immersion (which is the highest-leverage way to use it), the order of operations that pays back fastest is:

  1. Memorize the te-form decision tree. It's used everywhere. Once you can derive it instantly from the dictionary form, plain past comes free, and roughly a third of the grammar in §2 falls into place.
  2. Internalize the verb group rule. You need it for every conjugation that isn't the te-form. The "godan-in-disguise" exceptions (帰る, 切る, 走る, 入る, 知る, 要る) are worth flashcards on their own.
  3. Drill the eleven core particles from §3, plus the four conditionals from §2.8. These three together cover most sentence-level ambiguity you'll meet at N4-N3.
  4. Skim, don't memorize §5 (sentence patterns) and §6 (keigo). Both are pattern-recognition material — they'll click on the third or fourth encounter in real text, not on the page. Mark this reference with sticky tabs or scroll-to-section links and consult it when you meet something specific.
