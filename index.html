i+1
A brief primer

What "i+1" means

Stephen Krashen's input hypothesis — and why this tool exists.

The idea is simple: language is acquired when you understand input that's just slightly above your current level. Linguist Stephen Krashen called your current level i, and the sweet spot for growth i+1 — material with one new thing per sentence, comprehensible enough to follow, hard enough to stretch.

i (what you know) + 1 (one new piece) = acquisition

If input is too easy (i or below), you're enjoying yourself but not growing. If it's too hard (i+2 or beyond), you're decoding rather than acquiring — your brain doesn't have enough scaffolding to pick up the new pieces from context. The growth happens in a narrow band where you understand ~85–95% on first exposure and the gaps are guessable.

This tool helps you find that band. The level you input represents your comfortable comprehension zone (your i); the resources it recommends are picked from the band just above (your i+1). Mix both — comfortable content for fluency-building, stretch content for growth.

↓ Too easy
You're at i
100% comprehension, no new words or grammar. Fine for review and fluency, but you're not acquiring new language.
★ Just right
You're at i+1
85–95% comprehension. You catch the meaning, miss a few specifics, and pick up new pieces from context naturally.
↑ Too hard
You're at i+2+
Below ~80% — you're decoding word-by-word. Switch to easier input or use heavy scaffolding (subs, dictionary, re-listens).

⚠ Important caveats
  1. Proficiency is multi-dimensional. Vocabulary, grammar, listening speed, kanji recognition, and productive ability rarely sit at the same level for any one learner.
  2. JGR Sakura aims for ~95% vocabulary coverage at your recommended level — meaning that level is calibrated to your comfortable reading zone, not your stretch goal. The level immediately above is your i+1 growth edge.
  3. JGR's vocabulary test uses Japanese-Japanese synonym matching, which is stricter than English-glossed flashcard recognition. Learners with bilingual flashcard backgrounds may score lower than their actual recognition vocabulary would suggest.
  4. "1,000 words" on JGR is not the same as "1,000 words" on JLPT vocab lists — the sets overlap heavily but aren't identical. Conjugated forms and counting methods differ.
  5. The Duolingo Score scale runs 0–160 (mapped to CEFR up through C2), but Duolingo's course content currently caps at score 130 (end of B2). Scores above 130 aren't currently reachable through the app — they're scale headroom, not content.
  6. CEFR was designed for European languages. Its application to Japanese is a recent adaptation and remains an active research area — N5 ≈ A1 is a useful approximation, not an equivalence.
  7. The CIJ difficulty score is a statistical model of video transcripts. Topic familiarity, visual scaffolding, and personal interest can shift effective difficulty up or down by 10–15 points.
  8. Self-described JLPT levels on learner resources are rough. Tadoku Level 5 (claimed N2–N1) often reads closer to N3; Crystal Hunters' "natural" version (claimed N4–N3) is closer to N4. Use as a starting point, not a measurement.
  9. Resource freshness as of 2025–2026: NHK News Web Easy is now paywalled (NHK monetized its streaming service in 2025) — the free third-party mirror NHK Easier is recommended instead. News in Slow Japanese has been dormant since its last episode on January 31, 2025; the archive is still useful, but no new episodes are appearing.
